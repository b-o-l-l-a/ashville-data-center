## A village's infrastructure in distress

Earlier this year, the six-person Village Council of Ashville, Ohio was in a precarious position. On February 18th, the state's Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) issued a Notice of Violation to the village for several problems found during an inspection of Ashville's water infrastructure the previous month:

- A tank of sodium hypochlorite (bleach used to disinfect municipal water systems) was found to have a faulty or missing lid, allowing debris, insects and other contaminants into the tank.
- One of the Village's wells had been inoperable since August 2024, leaving the municipal water system without backup in case something happened to one of the other wells in the system.
- The soil beneath an elevated 300,000-gallon water tank had begun to sink, providing a low spot for water to collect and potentially causing further erosion around the base of the tower.

The Council knew their water infrastructure was in dire need of repairs and/or replacement. On February 2nd, two weeks prior to the EPA's Notice of Violation, all six members of Council voted in favor of awarding $12.84M to a company to build a new water treatment facility.

This was a tremendous expense - the entire Village's budget for water-related expenditures in 2020 was ~$1.23M. The water treatment plant renovation represented ~10x the amount of money the Village had available for an entire year, and didn't address all the violations specified by the EPA.

This confluence of events put extreme strain on Village Council and Ashville, home to ~4600 residents and the world's oldest traffic light. Because the EPA required a response within thirty days, the Council only had a few weeks to come up with some sort of solution.

## Financial Windfall

Eight days later, on February 26th, that solution presented itself. EdgeConneX — a global data center developer founded in 2009, backed by Swedish private equity firm EQT and operating facilities across four continents — offered the Village $35 million to address the improvements mandated by the Ohio EPA, as well as an additional $64M to the local school district, and $3M to a local fire department.

There was a catch, however – to get the funds, Council needed to approve the construction of a gigantic data center within Ashville's municipal boundaries, on 195 acres bordered by Weigand Road to the north and State Route 752 to the south, roughly a mile from the high school football stadium.

On April 6th, lacking many - or any - other options, the Council approved construction of the data center in a 5-1 vote.

## An emergency measure

Crucially, the April 6th resolution to approve the data center was also declared an "emergency measure", a bureaucratic designation that eliminated the possibility for the public to organize a petition to force a vote on the issue.

According to Ohio Code Section 731.29, any measure passed by municipal legislative authorities (e.g., the Ashville Village Council), a "petition may be filed within thirty days after the council has passed the ordinance…, ordering that such ordinance or measure be submitted to the electors of such municipal corporation for their approval or rejection".

Elsewhere, the code specifies that if a petition got enough signatures that exceeds 35% of the number of electors in the most recent gubernatorial election, then the resolution to approve or reject the data center would be put in the hands of a public vote.

However, since the resolution was designated as an emergency, it went into effect immediately, forgoing the 30-day period in which Ashville residents could have organized a petition.

## Bring your own power

The data center itself is gigantic, requiring 800 MW of electricity running continuously 24/7/365. That's enough capacity to comfortably provide the electricity requirements to power every home in Columbus, Ohio, a city with a population of 900,000.

The electric grid near the data center project site in Ashville can't support this additional load. So, on May 12th, a subsidiary of EdgeConneX, PowerConneX, filed an application with the Ohio Power Siting Board to build an 800MW natural gas plant on the same 195-acre plot as the planned data center.

The final decision from the Ohio Power Siting Board regarding the gas plant is expected around October 2026. This website is dedicated to stopping this project, while attempting to find ways in which Ashville and similar localities can fund critical infrastructure projects without needing to acquiesce to the whims of a global corporation with deep pockets.
